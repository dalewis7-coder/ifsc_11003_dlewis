Geography & Designation

River Course and Counties

Established by Act of Congress

The Buffalo National River, located in northern Arkansas, was the first National River to be designated in the United States. The Buffalo River is 153 miles (246 km) long. The lower 135 miles (217 km) flow within the boundaries of an area managed by the National Park Service. The river flows through Newton, Searcy, Marion, and Baxter Counties, from west to east.

The river originates in the highest part of the Boston Mountains of the Ozarks, flows out onto the Springfield Plateau near the historic community of Erbie, and finally crosses a portion of the Salem Plateau just before joining the White River. The Park is home to the state's only elk herd. The upper section of the river in the Ozark National Forest is managed by the U.S. Forest Service and is designated as a National Scenic River and a National Wild River.

The Buffalo National River was established by an Act of Congress on March 1, 1972, ending the recurring plans of the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers to construct one or more dams on the river. Three segments totaling 11,978 acres (48.47 km²) were designated the Buffalo National River Wilderness in 1975.

Key Facts About the Buffalo National River

  1. It is 153 miles (246 km) long in total.
  2. It was the first National River designated in the United States.
  3. It was established by Congress on March 1, 1972.
  4. It flows through Newton, Searcy, Marion, and Baxter Counties.
  5. The National Park Service headquarters is located in Harrison, Arkansas.

Key Terms

National River
A designation that protects natural rivers from industrial uses, impoundments, and other obstructions that may change the natural character of the river or disrupt habitat for flora and fauna.
Karst Topography
A landscape formed from soluble rocks such as limestone, featuring sinkholes, caves, springs, and dramatic bluffs — prominently found along the upper Buffalo River.
Hemmed-In-Hollow Falls
A 209-foot (64 m) waterfall accessible by a 0.65-mile hike, the highest of its kind between the Southern Appalachians and the Rockies.
Boston Mountains
The highest part of the Ozarks, where the Buffalo River originates before flowing eastward across northern Arkansas.

A Note on the River's Protection

The National River designation exists to keep the Buffalo free from industrial uses, impoundments and other obstructions that may change the natural character of the river or disrupt the natural habitat for the flora and fauna that live in or near the river.

"The upper section has most of the whitewater rapids to be found along the river, and features dramatic karst topography including sinkholes and caves, springs, and waterfalls, over 500-foot tall sandstone and limestone bluffs, and many rock formations."

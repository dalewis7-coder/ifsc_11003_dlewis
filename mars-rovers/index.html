Exploring the Red Planet

Since 1997, NASA has sent five rovers to the surface of Mars. These robotic explorers have transformed our understanding of the Red Planet and are paving the way for future human exploration.

About the Mars Rover Program

The Mars Exploration Program is a long-term effort by NASA to study Mars using orbiters, landers, and rovers. Rovers are especially valuable because they can move across the surface to study rocks, soil, and atmosphere up close.

Each mission has built on the lessons of the last. Rovers have grown larger and more capable over time. The program is managed by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) in Pasadena, California.

For more information, visit mars.nasa.gov.

Featured Rovers

Curiosity

Mission: Mars Science Laboratory

Launched: November 26, 2011

Landed: August 6, 2012 - Gale Crater

Curiosity is a car-sized rover that has been exploring Gale Crater since 2012. It confirmed that Mars once had conditions capable of supporting microbial life and remains active today.

Read more about Curiosity →

Perseverance

Mission: Mars 2020

Launched: July 30, 2020

Landed: February 18, 2021 - Jezero Crater

Perseverance is NASA's most advanced rover. It is searching for signs of ancient microbial life, caching rock samples for a future Earth return mission, and deployed the Ingenuity helicopter.

Read more about Perseverance →

Spirit and Opportunity

Mission: Mars Exploration Rover (MER)

Launched: June and July 2003

Landed: January 2004

Designed for 90 days, Spirit lasted over 6 years and Opportunity nearly 15. Together they drove over 45 km and confirmed that liquid water once existed on Mars.

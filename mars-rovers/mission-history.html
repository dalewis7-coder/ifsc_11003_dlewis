Mission History

A detailed look at each Mars rover mission, from goals and launch details to major discoveries and design highlights.

Curiosity (2011 - Present)

Mission Goals

Curiosity's primary goal is to determine whether Mars ever had conditions capable of supporting microbial life. It also studies the planet's geology, climate, and radiation environment to prepare for future human missions.

Launch and Landing

  • Launch date: November 26, 2011
  • Landing date: August 6, 2012
  • Landing site: Gale Crater, near Mount Sharp
  • Landing method: Sky crane descent system

Major Discoveries

  • Confirmed Gale Crater once held a freshwater lake with habitable chemistry
  • Detected complex organic molecules in 3-billion-year-old rock samples
  • Measured seasonal changes in atmospheric methane
  • Documented radiation levels important for planning crewed missions

Key Design Features

Curiosity weighs 899 kg and is powered by a nuclear RTG, so it does not rely on sunlight. It carries 17 cameras, a ChemCam laser for remote rock analysis, and a drill for collecting powdered rock samples. It remains active as of 2024.

Perseverance (2020 - Present)

Mission Goals

Perseverance is searching for signs of ancient microbial life. It is also caching rock and soil samples for a future Earth return mission and testing technology that will support future human exploration.

Launch and Landing

  • Launch date: July 30, 2020
  • Landing date: February 18, 2021
  • Landing site: Jezero Crater, an ancient lake delta
  • Landing method: Sky crane descent system

Major Discoveries

  • Successfully produced oxygen from the Martian atmosphere using the MOXIE instrument
  • Collected over 20 rock core samples sealed in tubes for future return to Earth
  • Deployed Ingenuity, which made the first powered flight on another planet
  • Identified organic-rich rock formations at a site called Hogwallow Flats

Key Design Features

Perseverance weighs 1,025 kg and shares Curiosity's basic design with significant upgrades. It has 23 cameras, a coring drill for intact rock samples, ground-penetrating radar called RIMFAX, and improved autonomous navigation. It also carried the Ingenuity helicopter to Mars.

Spirit and Opportunity (2003 - 2010 / 2019)

Mission Goals

Spirit and Opportunity were sent to study the history of water on Mars. Their goal was to find rocks and minerals that could only have formed in the presence of liquid water.

Launch and Landing

  • Spirit launch: June 10, 2003 | Landed: January 4, 2004 - Gusev Crater
  • Opportunity launch: July 7, 2003 | Landed: January 25, 2004 - Meridiani Planum
  • Landing method: Airbag bounce landing system

Major Discoveries

  • Opportunity found hematite "blueberries" that could only form in the presence of water
  • Spirit found silica-rich soil that required a wet environment to form
  • Both rovers confirmed that liquid water existed on ancient Mars
  • Opportunity drove over 45 km total, a surface record at the time

Key Design Features

Each rover weighed 185 kg and ran on solar panels. They carried a panoramic camera, microscopic imager, spectrometers, and a rock abrasion tool. Spirit became stuck in soft soil in 2009; Opportunity was lost to a global dust storm in 2018.

Mission Timeline

Key dates across the history of Mars rover exploration:

July 4, 1997

Sojourner Lands on Mars

The first Mars rover arrives at Ares Vallis aboard the Mars Pathfinder mission.

January 3, 2004

Spirit Lands at Gusev Crater

Spirit airbag-bounces to a landing and begins its 90-day mission.

January 25, 2004

Opportunity Lands at Meridiani Planum

Opportunity lands and quickly finds evidence of ancient water.

August 6, 2012

Curiosity Lands at Gale Crater

The sky crane delivers Curiosity after the famous "seven minutes of terror."

February 18, 2021

Perseverance Lands at Jezero Crater

Perseverance arrives with the Ingenuity helicopter attached to its belly.

April 19, 2021

Ingenuity's First Flight

Ingenuity makes the first powered, controlled flight on another planet.
